# Story Engine

## Age bands

### Ages 0-3
- 0-250 words, 12-24 pages.
- One idea, immediate familiarity, rhythmic repetition, concrete nouns.
- Very low peril; caregiver reassurance.
- Illustrations carry most meaning.

### Ages 3-5
- 250-700 words, typically 24-32 pages.
- One central problem and one clear emotional change.
- Short sentences, repetition with variation, predictable patterns.
- Humor, sound words, and visual surprises work well.

### Ages 4-7
- 500-1,000 words, typically 32 pages.
- Clear goal, three escalating attempts, emotional turning point, earned resolution.
- Rich read-aloud language with accessible context clues.
- Strong page turns and visual subtext.

### Ages 6-8 / early readers
- 800-2,000 words or leveled chapters.
- Controlled vocabulary, repeated high-frequency words, short paragraphs.
- More dialogue and independent problem solving.

### Ages 7-10 / illustrated chapter books
- 4,000-15,000 words.
- Chapters with mini-arcs, broader cast, subplot allowed.
- Emotional and logical continuity must be explicit.

### Ages 9-12 / middle grade
- 20,000-50,000 words unless user specifies shorter.
- Layered motivation, subplots, consequences, nuanced themes.
- Do not infantilize the voice.

## Concept matrix

Generate at least three concepts. Score each 1-10 on:
- child appeal
- emotional clarity
- visual potential
- originality
- age fit
- read-aloud quality
- series potential
- commercial title potential

Select the highest total unless a lower-scoring concept better matches a stated brief.

## Story bible

Include:
- one-sentence hook
- emotional promise
- theme stated as a question, not a sermon
- protagonist profile
- external desire
- internal need
- fear or mistaken belief
- supporting cast and functions
- setting rules
- story object or visual motif
- conflict ladder
- climax choice
- ending image
- repetition pattern
- vocabulary targets
- continuity ledger

## Picture-book plot architecture

1. Opening image and character identity.
2. Desire or problem appears quickly.
3. First attempt: plausible, small failure.
4. Second attempt: more inventive, bigger consequence.
5. Third attempt: emotional or comic escalation.
6. Low point: character confronts fear or mistaken belief.
7. Climax: character makes a meaningful choice.
8. Resolution: external result and internal growth.
9. Final image: emotionally satisfying callback.

## Page-turn design

End a page/spread with one of:
- a question
- an interrupted action
- a surprising discovery
- a visual reveal
- a repeated line with changed meaning
- a promise of consequence

Do not force suspense on every page. Alternate energetic spreads with breathing room.

## Read-aloud test

Revise when:
- two consecutive sentences have awkward stress patterns
- names are hard to pronounce without purpose
- repeated words are accidental rather than musical
- dialogue tags interrupt rhythm
- page text exceeds the visual capacity of the spread
- exposition explains what the image can show

## Rhyme rules

- Use rhyme only when meter is stable.
- Prefer near-perfect meaning over forced rhyme.
- Mark stressed beats and read aloud.
- Avoid inverted grammar used only to rhyme.
- If more than 10% of lines require repair, convert to rhythmic prose.

## Wordless books

For each spread specify:
- visible action
- emotional state
- cause-and-effect link
- focal point
- page-turn reveal
- recurring visual motif
- background subplot, if any

The sequence must be understandable without captions.
